## Bible idea: the page was born inside a scroll

Two pieces of history reframe the whole "flip or scroll?" debate:

- **Latin *pagina* — the word "page" — originally named one column of writing in a papyrus
  scroll.** An ancient reader unrolled with one hand and rolled up with the other, exposing
  one or two columns at a time. *Reading a scroll was a paged experience.* Smooth continuous
  scrolling is a modern web invention with no ancient precedent — so Scroll mode's horizontal
  columns are already secretly pages, and "flip vs. scroll" is a false dichotomy.
- **Early Christians were the codex's most aggressive early adopters.** Surviving early
  Christian scripture is overwhelmingly codices — bound pages — while contemporary Jewish and
  pagan literature stayed on scrolls. The codex is where annotation culture arrived: fixed
  pages you can hold open, flip back to, and write beside. Both formats are authentically
  part of this text's story — which is why an app named **bible-codex** offers both.

And one piece of modern research: readers use **spatial position as a memory cue** — "that
verse was top-left." Studies of paging vs. scrolling find paging readers re-find text faster
and build a better mental model; what scrolling *measurably* hurts is those spatial landmarks.
The key nuance: the research punishes **moving and reflowing** the landmarks, not scrolling
itself. A fixed canvas you scroll *over* keeps its geography; a reflowing text does not.

## App idea: Page = one chapter, at fixed geometry

The Codex <span class="term">Page</span> makes three commitments
([ADR-0016](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0016-codex-and-scroll-are-purpose-bound-reading-modes.md)):

1. **Fixed geometry.** A page is typeset like a *printed edition*: measure and typography
   fixed per preset. The device merely **letterboxes and scales** it — so rotation and
   changing devices are *viewing* operations. Ink cannot be orphaned by them, by
   construction. (Every serious ink app — GoodNotes, Notability — inks onto fixed pages for
   exactly this reason.)
2. **The page is a chapter.** Semantic pagination: the chapter *is* the page break, so **no
   page-break algorithm exists in v1** — no widows, orphans, or poem-splitting engine work.
   A short psalm fits one screen; Psalm 119 is one tall fixed canvas you scroll *within* the
   page. Your spatial landmarks stay valid, because the canvas never changes — only your
   window over it moves. Flip = chapter navigation.
3. **Margins expand outward only.** The <span class="term">Margin rail</span> is a
   first-class reserved region beside the text (the wide-margin *journaling Bible*,
   engineered). Widen it and the page grows **outward into letterbox space** — the text
   measure is never squeezed, so expansion can never reflow text or invalidate ink.

## And the pen: Rail ink, slotted to a verse

v1's only Ink is <span class="term">Rail ink</span>: freehand strokes living in the rail,
**verse-slotted**. The blob's *content* never distorts — it's your handwriting — but its
*slot* follows the verse. That buys an honest survival story:

- **Rotation / new device** → nothing happens at all (fixed geometry absorbs it).
- **Typography change** (font, preset) → the text re-typesets — effectively a **new
  edition** of the page — and rail ink **re-slots** beside its verse. This is the honest
  limit, stated plainly: fixed pages absorb rotation and device change, *not* typography
  change. It's also why `ink_annotation` now keys by **edition**, not by the old
  `(translation, scroll_mode, layout_hash)` triple.
- **In-text ink** (circling a word with the pen) would *not* survive a new edition — which
  is exactly why v1 scopes it out. It returns as a deliberate later phase carrying the full
  edition-bound fragility; meanwhile in-text decoration is <span class="term">Markup</span>'s
  job, which reflows and ports *by design*. The two physics, still respected.

<div class="callout">
  <span class="tag">A Lesson-3 callback — headings, still special</span>
  <p>Remember Psalm superscriptions ("A psalm of David")? In the <em>Hebrew</em> Bible many
  of them are canonical text — verse 1 itself — but in English tradition they're headings,
  whose Tokens carry <code>verse = NULL</code> and are <strong>not anchorable</strong>
  (Lesson 3). Rail ink is verse-slotted, so ink <em>beside a heading</em> has no verse to
  slot to — it waits on the deferred block-grain heading anchor (OVERVIEW §5). The old rule
  you learned is now load-bearing in a brand-new feature.</p>
</div>
