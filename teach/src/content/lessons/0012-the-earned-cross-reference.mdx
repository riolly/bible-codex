## Bible idea: a word that glows once you've earned it

You're reading **Genesis 1** — "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth." Later, books
on, you reach **John 1** — "In the beginning was the Word." If you've read *both*, the word
*"beginning"* in John quietly begins to **glow**. Follow it and you're **carried** back to Genesis
1:1, to read that first sentence again — now resonating against John's. The connection wasn't handed
to you in a footnote; you **earned** it by reading your way to it.

That's the feel the app is chasing: reading as an **adventure**, where a cross-reference is a
*treasure you discover*, not a citation you skim. "Challenge, not ease." Discovery is the reward.

## App idea: a Portal is a gate + a skin over a Cross-reference

Back in Phase 2 you met the **Connector** — *your* hand-drawn arrow between two passages. Its
read-only, shipped-with-the-app sibling is the <span class="term">Cross-reference</span>: an
**editorial link between two Anchors** (think the Treasury of Scripture Knowledge — "this verse
connects to that one"). It's already in the data model.

A **Portal** is that exact Cross-reference with **two thin wrappers that store nothing new**
([WISHLIST](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/WISHLIST.md)):

- **A gate — *when* it shows.** The link stays invisible until you've **earned** it: read the passage
  it connects to, or collected the clue that reveals it. That gate is just a read of
  **reading progress** — the same "what have you read" signal we'll lean on again in the next lesson.
- **A skin — *how* it feels.** When the gate opens, the link is *presented* as a glow on the word
  plus a transport to the other passage. Glow and transport are **presentation**, not data.
