## Bible idea: the same Bible, in every pocket

A paper Bible is one object — its marks go where it goes, and nowhere else. The app's promise is the
opposite: mark a verse on your **phone on the train**, and it's there on your **tablet at home** and
your **laptop at work** — even if you made the mark with no signal. And if you delete a note by
mistake, you can get it back. That "local-first, synced, undoable" experience is a set of demands,
and each one forces a storage decision.

## App idea: a sync-first, coordinate-anchored store

Because the user layer is **local-first, offline-editable, and synced across one user's devices**, it
diverges sharply from the read-only corpus ([ADR-0006](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0006-annotation-layer-is-a-sync-first-coordinate-anchored-store.md)):

- **Client-generated `UUIDv7` ids, not autoincrement.** Two devices creating marks offline can't be
  handed colliding numbers by a server they can't reach — so each row mints its own id locally. v7
  is **time-sortable**, giving free created-order and good index locality.
- **The layer never foreign-keys into the corpus.** It joins the corpus by stable **coordinate** —
  translation + `book.slug` + chapter + verse + word-index — exactly the <span class="term">Anchor</span>
  again. So when the corpus is **re-ingested** and its integer keys renumber, **nothing is orphaned**.
- **Deletes are soft-delete tombstones.** Every row carries `created_at` / `updated_at` /
  `deleted_at`. A hard `DELETE` can't propagate to an offline device (it never learns the row is
  gone); a tombstone can — and it **doubles as undo**.
- **Ink is opaque.** Its stroke points are packed as a `Float32` **BLOB** (never one row per point,
  never JSON) — strokes are replayed whole, never queried point-by-point — bound to a layout by
  `(translation, scroll_mode)` + a `layout_hash`.

One more piece of furniture: a <span class="term">Layer</span> — a named, toggleable group of marks
with a `visible` flag. It's the "notes on / off" switch, and it spans both Markup and Ink.
