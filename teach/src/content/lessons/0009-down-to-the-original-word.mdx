## Bible idea: open an interlinear

A reader who wants to go deeper than the English opens an **interlinear** Bible. Under (or over)
each English word sits the original — the **Greek** in the New Testament, the **Hebrew** in the Old
— usually wearing four little tags: a **Strong's number** (G3056 / H7225), the **dictionary form**
of the word, a tiny **parsing code**, and a one-word **gloss**. That whole apparatus is the surface
of Phase 3, printed on paper. (We name those four tags in the next lesson; this one is about the
layer they hang on.)

Two things jump out the moment you read an interlinear closely:

- **Translations don't agree word-for-word.** "In the beginning" is **three** English words sitting
  over **two** Greek words (*Ἐν ἀρχῇ* — literally "In beginning"). One Greek word can blossom into
  three English ones; three English words can collapse into one Greek word. The link is
  **many-to-many**, never a tidy 1:1.
- **Hebrew packs several "words" into one.** Hebrew glues a conjunction, an article, a preposition,
  a stem, and a suffix into a single written word. *bərēʾšîṯ* ("in the beginning") is really
  *bə-* ("in") + *rēʾšîṯ* ("beginning") — and a scholar splits it into those **morphemes**, each
  with its own Strong's, dictionary form, and gloss.

## App idea: the Original Word hub + alignment

That underneath layer is the <span class="term">Original Word</span> hub
([ADR-0007](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0007-original-word-hub-is-a-morpheme-grained-externally-keyed-bridge.md)).
Three ideas build it:

- **An Original Word is a morpheme-occurrence, not always a whole word.** It's one lexical
  <span class="term">Segment</span> as it appears at one spot in the text — a *whole word* in Greek,
  a *morpheme* in Hebrew. So *bə-* and *rēʾšîṯ* are **two** Original Words; the
  <span class="term">Written Word</span> *bərēʾšîṯ* is reconstructed by ordering its sibling segments
  (`segment_of` + `segment_index`). One uniform entity means alignment, gloss, and morphology all
  point at one kind of thing — no special "morpheme" code path.
- **Alignment is the many-to-many bridge.** <span class="term">Alignment</span> maps a translation's
  <span class="term">Tokens</span> to Original Words — N Tokens ↔ M Original Words. It's the fine,
  word-grain edge between a spoke and the hub.
- **A head, plus the full set.** Each Token also carries a single `token.ow_id` — the **head**, "the
  one Original Word this Token primarily maps to." That denormalized column is the fast path and the
  portability handle; the full many-to-many lives in the `interlinear_alignment` junction. The
  reverse view — a translation's words shown *under* the original — is **derived** by walking
  alignment, never stored.
