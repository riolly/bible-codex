## Bible idea: the Masoretes gave each margin a different job

Between roughly the 6th and 10th centuries, Jewish scribe-scholars called the **Masoretes**
guarded the Hebrew Bible's text with a web of marginal notes — the **Masorah** ("tradition").
Their notes came in two kinds, and they *never* mixed the two margins:

- **Masorah parva** ("small") — terse, abbreviation-dense guard-notes ("this word occurs
  3 times spelled this way") — written in the **side margins**, right beside the word they
  guard.
- **Masorah magna** ("large") — the expansive lists and cross-references backing those
  notes — written in the **top and bottom margins**, where there is room to stretch out.

Same tradition, two margin regions, two content grains: **side = short and word-adjacent;
top/bottom = long and passage-level.** Medieval Christian manuscripts made the same move
(the *Glossa Ordinaria*: word-glosses between the lines, passage commentary in the margin),
and the Talmud page is the extreme case — concentric zones, each a different voice. The
margin's *position* encoded the annotation's *kind*. That's a thousand-year-old design
convention, not a workaround.

## App idea: don't transform the margins — assign them

Here's the problem that forced this. <span class="term">Ink</span> is layout-bound (you know
this from Lesson 6). A vertical layout has **left/right** margin space; a horizontal-column
layout has **top/bottom** margin space. Ink drawn in one has no honest home in the other —
the spaces don't transform into each other. And on a tablet, **rotation** forces the question
*daily*.

[ADR-0016](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0016-codex-and-scroll-are-purpose-bound-reading-modes.md)
answers with the Masoretes' move: **don't transform — assign.** The two layouts stop being
interchangeable orientations of one surface and become two **purpose-bound modes**, each
hard-bound to a device orientation:

- **📖 Codex mode (portrait)** — the ***study*** surface. Paged like a physical Bible; home
  of *all* annotation — Markup, Notes, and ink — in and around a **side margin rail** (the
  Masorah *parva* position: short, word-adjacent).
- **📜 Scroll mode (landscape)** — the ***journey*** surface. Continuous horizontal columns,
  immersive reading, Portals, progress. Its top/bottom bands carry passage-level and meta
  content (the Masorah *magna* position) — and it stays **clean** in v1: no ink ever, no
  Markup authoring yet.

<div class="callout key">
  <span class="tag">Rotation becomes a meaningful gesture</span>
  <p>Upright = <em>"I'm studying."</em> Sideways = <em>"I'm reading."</em> And the
  rotation-orphans-ink problem doesn't get <em>mitigated</em> — it <strong>cannot occur</strong>:
  ink exists only in Codex mode, whose page geometry doesn't reflow on rotation. The old open
  question ("what's Ink's reflow policy?") isn't answered; it's <strong>dissolved</strong>.</p>
</div>

This is the project's **two-physics discipline** one level up. Every good decision in this
blueprint has the same shape: when two things have different physics, refuse to pretend
they're one thing (Markup/Ink, corpus/user-data, rules/computed-layout). Transforming
side-margin ink into top-margin ink would be pretending. Two deliberate refusals worth
knowing: the **inverse assignment** (study in landscape) was rejected — writing posture is
portrait (every notebook and journaling Bible), and landscape's wide sweep is exactly what
makes multi-column feel like a scroll; and **free mode choice in any orientation** was
rejected — the moment both modes exist in both orientations, the orphaning problem
un-dissolves. One naming rule: the modes are named by *purpose*, never by pixels — say
**Codex mode** and **Scroll mode**, not "vertical/horizontal mode."
