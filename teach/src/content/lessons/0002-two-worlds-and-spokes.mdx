## Bible idea: why there are many translations

The Bible was written in ancient **Hebrew** (Old Testament) and **Greek** (New Testament).
Everything you read in English is a **translation** — a team's rendering of that original into
English. There are many because teams make different choices:

- **KJV** (1611) — historic, formal, "thee/thou" English.
- **NASB** — very literal, word-for-word.
- **BSB** (Berean Standard Bible) — modern, readable, openly licensed.

Same passage, different words. "John 1:1" exists in all of them — but the actual English
*words*, and even how many words, differ. Hold onto that: **the passage is shared; the words
are not.**

## App idea 1: two data worlds that never mix

bible-codex keeps two completely separate stores:

<figure>
  <TwoWorlds />
  <figcaption>The text is never edited; your marks are never baked into the text. They meet only
  at a <span class="term">scripture coordinate</span> — the Anchor from Lesson 1.</figcaption>
</figure>

This separation is *why* the Anchor exists. Because your marks live in their own world and only
*point* into the text by coordinate, the whole Bible text can be swapped or re-imported and your
marks don't even notice.

## App idea 2: translations are spokes, never wired together

Here's the rule that trips people up. A **KJV word** and a **BSB word** are *different objects*.
The app **never** draws a direct line from "this KJV word" to "that BSB word" — the word counts
and order don't line up, so no such clean 1:1 line exists.

Instead, translations are **spokes** that connect only through two shared **hubs**:

- **Canonical Verse** — the *coarse* hub. Links translations by **passage** ("John 1:1 in KJV"
  ↔ "John 1:1 in BSB").
- **Original Word** — the *fine* hub. Links translations by the underlying **Greek/Hebrew word**
  they both translate. (This is Phase 3.)

  <p>A highlight you draw in KJV reappears when you switch to BSB <strong>not</strong> because

  passage always, and by exact word once Phase 3's Original Word hub lights up.</p>
