## Bible idea: the scribes counted every word — because texts drift

Back to the Masoretes one last time. Their margin notes weren't commentary — they were
**integrity checks**: *this word occurs exactly 3 times spelled this way; the middle word of
the Torah is this one; this verse has 42 letters.* They counted words and letters so that a
copyist's drift — one inserted word, one changed spelling — would be **caught against the
count** instead of silently propagating into every future copy. The Masorah was, in software
terms, a checksum on scripture.

Texts still drift. Modern translations publish **textual revisions** — the BSB (one of the
app's two launch translations) is actively maintained and issues corrections. And there's a
subtler drift nobody publishes: **what counts as one word**. Is *LORD's* one word or two? Is
*father-in-law* one word or three? Different answers renumber every word that follows.

## App idea 1: an edition stamp + a quote witness (ADR-0013)

Suppose a BSB revision inserts one word early in a verse you've highlighted. Your anchor
says `word-index 4` — but word 4 is now a *different word*. No error, no orphan: every
affected mark **silently slides onto the wrong words**, discovered months later. Three
additions close the gap
([ADR-0013](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0013-corpus-text-is-versioned-and-markup-carries-a-quote-witness.md)):

1. **`translation.edition`** — an identifier stamped on the corpus at ingest. Now "which
   text was this mark minted against?" has an answer.
2. **A quote witness on every Markup anchor** — the *surface text of the anchored words*
   stored beside the coordinate (`mark.target_quote`, `note.pin_quote`,
   `binding.anchor_quote`), plus the edition it was minted against. The coordinate remains
   the anchor; the quote is redundant **verification** data — a Masorah note beside your own
   mark. (It's the W3C Web Annotation *TextQuoteSelector* pattern, as used by Hypothes.is.)
3. **A consent-gated reconciliation pass** when the app first runs against a changed
   edition: marks whose quote still matches pass untouched (the overwhelming majority); a
   mismatch **auto-repairs only when the quote matches exactly once within the same verse**;
   anything else is flagged *displaced* into a review list — surfaced honestly, never
   guessed across verses.

Why not verify at render time, forever? Because an edition change is a **rare, detectable
event** — reconcile once, not on every frame. And why no witness on Ink? Rail ink is already
edition-bound by its key (Lesson 16); any text revision invalidates that key anyway.

## App idea 2: the tokenizer is part of the locked seam (ADR-0014)

The second drift is self-inflicted. `word-index` means "the ordinal of a *word* Token,
punctuation excluded" — but *what counts as a word* is decided by the ingest tokenizer. So
the tokenization rules are **part of the meaning of every anchor coordinate**. Change a rule
("let's split *LORD's* differently") and every later index in affected verses shifts —
exactly like a text revision, except we did it to ourselves.
[ADR-0014](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0014-tokenization-policy-is-part-of-the-locked-anchor-seam.md)
declares migration-fatal seam #1 to **include the tokenization policy**:

- The word/punct rules are **registered, versioned corpus policy** — never implicit in
  ingester code, where a well-meaning "tokenizer bugfix" would ship an anchor migration
  nobody noticed.
- **Any rule change is an anchor migration**, treated with the gravity of changing the
  anchor columns themselves — with ADR-0013's reconciliation as the safety net (one
  mechanism guards both failure classes).
- The ingest golden fixtures are **adversarial**: possessives, hyphenated compounds,
  elisions, numbers, mixed punctuation — so rule drift fails tests *loudly*, not silently in
  shipped anchors.

There's even a payoff hiding in an old choice: word-indexes **exclude punctuation** — and
real-world revisions touch punctuation (modernized quotes, commas) far more often than
words. The existing rule makes anchors immune to the most common class of edition churn.
