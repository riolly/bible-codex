## Bible idea: marking a physical study Bible

Give a reader a paper study Bible for a few years and it fills with layers. Four moves cover almost
all of it:

- **Underline or highlight** a phrase — "these words matter."
- **Write a margin note** — your own words, beside the verse.
- **Draw an arrow** joining two verses that speak to each other.
- **Doodle freehand** in the margin — a sketch, a bracket, a scribble only you can read.

Now the pain. Buy a **different translation**, or a **large-print edition**, and every one of those
marks is stranded — they were tied to *that* page, that printing. The Module 0 promise was that
**your marks survive**. Phase 2 is where the app delivers that for annotations.

And notice two *felt* categories already. A clean underline or arrow is **semantic** — it means
"emphasise these words," and it would mean the same thing in any edition. A freehand pen scribble is
**expressive** — its whole value *is* the exact hand-drawn strokes. That difference is the seed of
the app's split.

## App idea: two classes, two physics

bible-codex stores marks as two classes with opposite physics ([ADR-0002](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0002-two-annotation-classes-markup-first.md)):

- <span class="term">Markup</span> stores a **reference** — `{ target, style }` — and lets the app
  **re-render** it. Because it's a reference and not pixels, it **reflows** when you change the
  font, **ports** across translations (its Anchor resolves in the new one), is **queryable** by
  research mode, **syncs as tiny rows**, and **runs in a browser**. Underline, highlight, box,
  circle, strike, arrow, and the margin note all live here.
- <span class="term">Ink</span> stores the **strokes themselves** — freehand pen captured over
  *one* translation's rendered layout. That's the physical-Bible feel, the product's emotional
  differentiator. But it's **glued to that layout**: not portable across translations, replayed
  whole rather than queried, and **tablet-only**.

So the build order falls out of the physics: **Markup is built first** (it works on web and desktop,
and it's the class that genuinely reflows and ports); **Ink is a later, tablet-native phase**.
