## Bible idea: whose Greek is it?

The English translations are one kind of data — the app ships them. But the Greek and Hebrew
*underneath*, with their Strong's numbers and parsings, are **somebody else's scholarship**:
maintained corpora like MACULA, STEPBible, or the Open Scriptures Hebrew Bible. That has two
consequences a paper interlinear never worries about:

- **You may want to swap the source.** A better-aligned corpus appears, or one you're using changes
  its licence. You should be able to re-source the original-language layer without rebuilding the
  reading text.
- **It must be licensable to redistribute.** This is the **same discipline as the NASB lesson** from
  the data-sources note: you can only bundle what you're allowed to ship. The reference data has to
  be chosen for its licence, not just its quality.

## App idea: opaque ids and three tiers

Two storage decisions make the layer swappable
([ADR-0007](https://github.com/riolly/bible-codex/blob/main/docs/adr/0007-original-word-hub-is-a-morpheme-grained-externally-keyed-bridge.md) #1, #4):

- **`ow_id` is an opaque *external* id — never self-minted, never parsed.** It comes straight from
  the maintained corpus. We don't invent our own word numbering (re-segmenting Hebrew would renumber
  it, and `ow_id` is migration-fatal — every word-grain Mark and Note since Phase 2 would orphan).
  Because we never *parse* it, swapping corpora is just a remap table. This is the Anchor's old
  refusal — "don't store something that renumbers under you" — applied to word ids.
- **Three tiers, joined by stable string keys; a real FK only *within* a tier.** The data is
  `corpus → hub → lexicon`. Anything crossing a boundary — `token.ow_id`,
  `alignment.original_word_id`, `original_word.lemma_id` / `strong_id` / `parse_code` — is a string
  key with **no foreign key**. The only real FK is corpus-internal (`alignment.token_id → token.id`),
  and it regenerates on every ingest, exactly like `block_id` did in Phase 1.

<figure>
  <ThreeTiers />
  <figcaption>Corpus, hub, and lexicon are three tiers joined by string keys, with no foreign key
  across a boundary. The only real FK lives inside the corpus tier. So the lexicon versions and
  re-sources on its own — and can even ship as a separate attachable file.</figcaption>
</figure>

That seam is the **same idea you met in Phase 2** — the user layer joining the corpus by coordinate
instead of a foreign key — now applied across three tiers. The payoff: the lexicon and hub
**version independently** of the reading text. Re-ingest the corpus and the string keys still point;
re-source Strong's and nothing in the corpus moves.

<div class="callout">
  <span class="tag">Teaching honesty — the real open questions (OVERVIEW §5)</span>
  <p>Naming the open risk is honest teaching, not a gap. Three are worth holding:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Sourcing is the open risk, not the schema.</strong> A redistributable interlinear
    corpus still has to be <em>chosen and licence-checked</em> — the schema is ready; the data isn't
    picked.</li>
    <li><strong>"Lemma is the spine" depends on the source.</strong> If a corpus ships Strong's but
    no lemma id, <code>lemma_id</code> is null at launch and statistics hang off <code>strong_id</code>
    instead — additive, no migration.</li>
    <li><strong>There is no DB-enforced cross-tier integrity.</strong> Because the cross-tier links
    are string keys, the database can't enforce them. An <strong>ingest-time validation pass</strong>
    must assert every <code>ow_id</code> / <code>strong_id</code> / <code>lemma_id</code> /
    <code>parse_code</code> resolves. That's the price of separability.</li>
  </ul>
</div>
