## Start with a physical Bible

You read a study Bible. You **underline** a phrase, scribble a **note** in the margin, draw an
**arrow** joining two verses. Now three things change:

- you make the **font bigger**,
- you switch to a **different translation** (different English wording),
- you turn the book **sideways** and read it as a scroll.

On paper, your marks would now sit in the wrong place, or be meaningless. bible-codex makes one
core promise: **your marks survive all of that.** That promise is the source of most of the
design decisions you'll meet later.

## The trick: what a mark *remembers*

It comes down to what gets stored when you make a mark.

- A naïve app stores *where on the screen* the mark sat: `{x: 340, y: 88, w: 120}`. Change the
  font and that box now sits over the wrong words. Dead.
- bible-codex stores *which words* the mark is on: *"the word **beginning** in John, chapter 1,
  verse 1."* Change anything about the layout and the app **re-finds** those words and re-draws
  the mark. Alive.

<div class="callout key">
  <span class="tag">The keystone term</span>
  <p>That stored scripture address is called an <span class="term">Anchor</span>. Every
  note, highlight, drawing, bookmark, and cross-reference hangs off one. Memorise this
  word — the next six lessons all lean on it.</p>
</div>

## The Bible literacy you need (just one thing today)

How do humans already point at a spot in the Bible? With a three-part address:

<p style="text-align:center; font-size:1.3rem;">
  <strong>John 3:16</strong> = book <strong>John</strong> · chapter <strong>3</strong> · verse <strong>16</strong>
</p>

That is the universal coordinate system every Bible uses. bible-codex adopts it exactly and adds
one more number — **which word** in the verse, called the <span class="term">word-index</span>.
So an Anchor is, in essence, `book · chapter · verse · word`.

<div class="callout">
  <span class="tag">One strict rule (we'll use it in Lesson 3)</span>
  <p>The <span class="term">word-index</span> counts <strong>only real words, not
  punctuation</strong>, and starts at <strong>0</strong>. So in <em>"In the
  beginning…"</em> → <code>In</code>=0, <code>the</code>=1, <code>beginning</code>=2.</p>
</div>

## Why this is the keystone (the payoff)

Because marks are addressed by scripture coordinate instead of pixels, they automatically:

- **reflow** when font, spacing, or typography change;
- **port** to other translations — the words still exist, just rendered differently;
- **sync** between tablet and desktop as a few bytes of coordinate, not a picture;
- survive the entire Bible text being **re-imported** from scratch — not one mark breaks.

This is locked as decision #1 of three "never change these" rules in the blueprint. Everything
else in the repo is allowed to move; this is not.
