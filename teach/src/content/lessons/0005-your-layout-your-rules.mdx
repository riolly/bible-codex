## Bible idea: every reader sets the page differently

People read physical Bibles differently. One wants a large face and generous margins for
margin-notes; another wants tight prose and deeply-indented poetry. The **words don't change** —
only how they're *set*. A good reader's Bible lets the reading be tuned without ever altering the
text.

## App idea: presentation is a *rules* layer, not pixels

bible-codex stores presentation as a small **rules** layer (ADR-0004), in two parts:

- **Global `reading_settings`** — scroll mode (horizontal / vertical) and theme.
- **A typography cascade** — `layout_preset` plus `layout_override` rows for spacing, indent,
  font, margins.

The decisive rule: every override **binds by a stable semantic key** — `genre`, `role`,
`book.slug`, or a canonical coordinate — **never by `token.id`, never by pixels**. And overrides
resolve in a cascade, **most specific wins**:

> base preset  <  genre  <  role  <  book

So "indent poetry a little more" attaches to *genre = poetry*, and "give the Psalms extra line
spacing" attaches to *book = Psalms* — and the book rule wins where they overlap. (`scroll_mode`
and `theme` are global, outside the cascade.)

What about the actual **line breaks and per-token rectangles** the engine paints? Those are the
**computed layout** — a *derived, ephemeral, local cache*. The engine re-typesets a whole chapter
on open (the prototype proved this at ~120fps), so the computed layout is **never synced and
never the source of truth**. Storing pixel positions would re-create exactly the orphaning the
Anchor was invented to avoid.

  canonical <em>coordinate</em>, never pixels; a layout rule addresses typography by semantic
  <em>key</em>, never pixels. Both refuse to store screen positions — so both survive corpus
  re-ingest, font changes, scroll-mode switches, and translation swaps with <strong>no
  migration</strong>.</p>
