Lesson 1 · The keystone

The Anchor

Why your marks survive a font change, a sideways scroll, and even a different translation — and why this one idea holds up the whole app.

Why this lesson

You set out to understand both the biblical concepts and how bible-codex is built. Almost every architectural decision in the repo traces back to the idea in this lesson. Learn it first and the rest of the blueprint stops feeling arbitrary.

Start with a physical Bible

You read a study Bible. You underline a phrase, scribble a note in the margin, draw an arrow joining two verses. Now three things change:

  • you make the font bigger,
  • you switch to a different translation (different English wording),
  • you turn the book sideways and read it as a scroll.

On paper, your marks would now sit in the wrong place, or be meaningless. bible-codex makes one core promise: your marks survive all of that. That promise is the source of most of the design decisions you'll meet later.

The trick: what a mark remembers

It comes down to what gets stored when you make a mark.

  • A naïve app stores where on the screen the mark sat: {x: 340, y: 88, w: 120}. Change the font and that box now sits over the wrong words. Dead.
  • bible-codex stores which words the mark is on: "the word beginning in John, chapter 1, verse 1." Change anything about the layout and the app re-finds those words and re-draws the mark. Alive.
The keystone term

That stored scripture address is called an Anchor. Every note, highlight, drawing, bookmark, and cross-reference hangs off one. Memorise this word — the next six lessons all lean on it.

The Bible literacy you need (just one thing today)

How do humans already point at a spot in the Bible? With a three-part address:

John 3:16  =  book John · chapter 3 · verse 16

That is the universal coordinate system every Bible uses. bible-codex adopts it exactly and adds one more number — which word in the verse, called the word-index. So an Anchor is, in essence, book · chapter · verse · word.

One strict rule (we'll use it in Lesson 3)

The word-index counts only real words, not punctuation, and starts at 0. So in "In the beginning…" → In=0, the=1, beginning=2.

Why this is the keystone (the payoff)

Because marks are addressed by scripture coordinate instead of pixels, they automatically:

  • reflow when font, spacing, or scroll direction change;
  • port to other translations — the words still exist, just rendered differently;
  • sync between tablet and desktop as a few bytes of coordinate, not a picture;
  • survive the entire Bible text being re-imported from scratch — not one mark breaks.

This is locked as decision #1 of three "never change these" rules in the blueprint. Everything else in the repo is allowed to move; this is not.

From memory, in one sentence: what is an Anchor, and what does it deliberately not store?
An Anchor is a scripture coordinate — book · chapter · verse · word-index — that a mark points to. It deliberately stores no pixels / no canvas position, so the position is recomputed for whatever layout is on screen.
