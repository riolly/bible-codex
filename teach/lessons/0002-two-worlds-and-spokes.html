Module 0 · Overview · the other keystone

Two worlds & spokes

Where the Bible text ends and "your stuff" begins — and why the app refuses to wire two translations directly together.

Where this sits

Lesson 1 gave you the Anchor — the coordinate a mark points to. This lesson gives you the two facts that the Anchor exists to serve: there are two separate data worlds, and translations are spokes that never touch. Together with the Anchor, that's the whole Overview.

Bible idea: why there are many translations

The Bible was written in ancient Hebrew (Old Testament) and Greek (New Testament). Everything you read in English is a translation — a team's rendering of that original into English. There are many because teams make different choices:

  • KJV (1611) — historic, formal, "thee/thou" English.
  • NASB — very literal, word-for-word.
  • BSB (Berean Standard Bible) — modern, readable, openly licensed.

Same passage, different words. "John 1:1" exists in all of them — but the actual English words, and even how many words, differ. Hold onto that: the passage is shared; the words are not.

This separation is why the Anchor exists. Because your marks live in their own world and only point into the text by coordinate, the whole Bible text can be swapped or re-imported and your marks don't even notice.

App idea 2: translations are spokes, never wired together

Here's the rule that trips people up. A KJV word and a BSB word are different objects. The app never draws a direct line from "this KJV word" to "that BSB word" — the word counts and order don't line up, so no such clean 1:1 line exists.

Instead, translations are spokes that connect only through two shared hubs:

  • Canonical Verse — the coarse hub. Links translations by passage ("John 1:1 in KJV" ↔ "John 1:1 in BSB").
  • Original Word — the fine hub. Links translations by the underlying Greek/Hebrew word they both translate. (This is Phase 3.)
The payoff (this is the Anchor lesson, completed)

A highlight you draw in KJV reappears when you switch to BSB not because the two are wired together — but because your mark ports through a hub: by passage always, and by exact word once Phase 3's Original Word hub lights up.

From memory: a highlight drawn in KJV shows up after you switch to BSB. By what path does it travel — and what path does it not take?
It ports through a shared hub — the Canonical Verse (by passage, coarse) and, in Phase 3, the Original Word (by exact word, fine). It does not travel by any direct KJV-word ↔ BSB-word link — translations are spokes, with no token-to-token edge.
